Criminal Procedure: The Regime of Powers in the CPC and the Venezuelan COPP

Category: Criminal Procedure

Enabled procedural acts and private accusation: With a specially granted power of attorney, the victim's representative can access the investigation file, request the practice of procedures, attend hearings, exercise the right to reply and, in crimes of private action, file a complaint or accusation on behalf of their represented party.

I. Requirement of Article 406 COPP

Article 406 of the COPP goes further: it requires that the private accusation identify the accused with precision —name, surnames, identification data— and describe the punishable act in a clear and detailed manner.

Individualized accused: Full name, identity card or other data that allow their unequivocal identification.

Delimited punishable act: Detailed description of time, mode, and place, as well as the proposed legal qualification. Enabling special power: The representative must prove that their represented party specifically authorized them for that act. Without this requirement, the accusation is inadmissible.

II. Differences between civil power and criminal power

The criminal representation of the victim does not admit generic formulas. Every relevant act requires a clear authorization. There are no shortcuts that do not lead to inadmissibility.

Purpose of the mandate

In the Civil Process (CPC) they are patrimonial rights and available claims. In the Criminal Process (COPP) they are procedural rights of the victim; public or private action.

Level of specificity

In civil, general for impulse; special for acts of disposition. In criminal, special for all acts of relevant intervention.

Nature of the litigation

Available in civil (the parties can waive, transact, and compromise). Predominantly unavailable in criminal (the State is the owner of the public action).

Consequence of insufficiency

In civil, ineffectiveness of the act of disposition; the process can continue. In criminal, inadmissibility or nullity; it can compromise the entire process.

III. Why the special power is indispensable in the representation of the victim

The requirement of a special power in the criminal representation of the victim responds to three fundamentals of prime importance:

  • Principle of procedural legality: In the criminal process, form is not an empty rite, but a bilateral guarantee. Society and justice bodies have the right to verify that the victim has acted within the limits of their mandate.
  • Legal certainty: The special power is the instrument that objectifies the victim's will and makes it verifiable by the court and other parties.
  • Risk of absolute nullities: Actions taken without sufficient power can lead to nullities that are not retroactively remediable.

In economic crime processes, where prescription periods are decisive, a nullity due to a defect in representation can translate into the irreversible loss of the right of action.

IV. Practical consequences and recommendations

Draft criminal powers with technical precision. The power to represent a victim in a criminal process must clearly identify the type of crime or the specific case. Distinguish civil power from criminal power in corporate representation instruments. Verify the sufficiency of the power before any relevant action. Act with specialized criminal advice from the first moment.

V. Corporate protocols and Compliance

Include the special power in the criminal compliance protocol. Companies with regulatory compliance programs should incorporate the provision of granting specific criminal powers into their crisis management protocols, with the sufficiency parameters that the COPP demands.

VI. Conclusion

The regime of powers in the Venezuelan process is not uniform: it obeys different logics depending on the nature of the process. This difference is not merely doctrinal. It has direct consequences on the validity of actions, the admissibility of briefs, and the effectiveness of representation at the most critical moment of the process.
